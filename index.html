Catalog of Concerns for Plant Health in Space-based Horticulture Systems

Willkommen

This tool was developed as part of a Master's thesis titled A Multidisciplinary Framework for Managing Plant Health in Extraterrestrial Horticulture by Saskia Weser at University of applied sciences in Dresden, Germany in cooperation with the German Aerospace Center, Institue of Space Systems, Department Systems Analysis Space Segment (SRS) in Bremen, Germany.

The primary goal of this catalog is to promote and safeguard plant health in space-based cultivation systems by providing a structured overview of potential hazards, underlying mechanisms, and mitigation strategies. It aims to enable mission planners and system designers to prevent failures, enhance system reliability, and maintain functional plant performance under extraterrestrial conditions.

In this context, plant health is defined as the condition that enables plants to fulfill their intended roles within a mission, such as food production, oxygen generation, waste recycling or psychological support for crew members.

Key indicators of plant health include:

  • Consistent growth
  • Yield performance
  • Physiological quality
  • Absence of disease
  • Environmental resilience

This catalog is designed to support:

  • Structured risk assessment in controlled horticultural environments
  • The design of robust and mission-adapted cultivation systems
  • Training of crew members and system operators
  • Resource planning and estimation of essential life support contributions
  • Onboard diagnosis and troubleshooting by astronauts during missions

The catalog is organized in a hierarchical structure to reflect different specificity levels. Each level contributes to a detailed and modular understanding of plant health concerns in controlled or extraterrestrial environments.

Hazard → A broad category of risk source that can impact plant health in many ways
└── Subhazard → A specific risk type or source within a hazard
    └── Concern → A specific documented or theoretical plant health issue
        ├── Concern ID + Title → Unique identifier that encodes catalog location and summarizes the issue
        ├── Affected System Component → Where the issue occurs or impacts
        ├── Description of the Concern → What the issue looks like in practice
        ├── Underlying Mechanism → Biological/technical cause-and-effect link
        ├── Source of the Concern → Based on literature, inference, or observation
        ├── Preventive Measures → How to prevent the issue
        └── Reactive Measures → How to correct the issue when it occurs

This structure ensures that every potential plant health issue is captured in a consistent, evidence-informed, and actionable way.

Hazard 1: Horticultural Management
Subhazard 1.1: Improper environmental management
Improper light exposure
Concern ID: HM-001 — No bloom development or unwanted bloom
  • Affected System Component: Shoot apex and apical meristem
  • Description of the Concern: Suboptimal photoperiod or light spectrum in combination with suboptimal temperatures can lead to unwanted bloom in leafy crops or no bloom in fruity crops.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Photoperiodic, spectral and/or temperature stress disrupting hormonal balance and developmental signaling (flower induction).
  • Source of the Concern: Experienced, documented by Rajapakse and Shahak 2018 , Thomas 2006 , Wu et al. 2025 .
  • Preventive Measures: Provide light arrangements for every species individually if they have different needs.
  • Reactive Measures: Adjust crop specific light settings; Cut unwanted bloom. Problem might also be temperature induced. Replant with correct light settings from the beginning if no improvement observed.

Concern ID: HM-002 — Intumescence
  • Affected System Component: Typically underside of leaves, stems and petioles
  • Description of the Concern: Swelling of plant tissue, looks blister-like and corky.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Imbalance in water regulation and cellular expansion in subepidermal tissues, triggered by a lack of adequate UV radiation.
  • Source of the Concern: Experienced, documented by Morrow and Wheeler 1996, Schuerger et al. 2022 and Williams et al. 2016.
  • Preventive Measures: Provide an adequate source of UV radiation (no high-pressure lamps); Use UV transmitting materials (e.g. Plexiglas); Select varieties that are less susceptible to intumescence development.
  • Reactive Measures: Switch lighting source; Reselect varieties with low susceptibility.

Picture source: ephytia.en accessed 07/2025

Concern ID: HM-003 — Leaf scorch or burns
  • Affected System Component: Leaves
  • Description of the Concern: Damage to leaf tissue due to excessive light intensity.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Photothermal injury exceeding plant tolerance thresholds.
  • Source of the Concern: Experienced
  • Preventive Measures: Provide species-specific and optimal light settings, ensure proper space between light source and plant.
  • Reactive Measures: Reduce light intensity; Repositionate lamps to create more space to plant.

Picture source: Own photo taken in 09/2024

Concern ID: HM-004 — Chlorosis and stunted growth
  • Affected System Component: Leaves and whole plant habitus
  • Description of the Concern: Pale, yellow leaves and stunted growth due to incorrect spectrum, excessive irradiance, or continuous light.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Impaired chlorophyll synthesis and light-induced metabolic imbalance.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Morrow and Wheeler 1996.
  • Preventive Measures: Provide species-specific lighting; Avoid continuous lighting and avoid static temperature inside plant module.
  • Reactive Measures: Adjust light settings.

Picture source: Cakmak 2013

Concern ID: HM-005 — Reduced leaf enlargement and stem elongation
  • Affected System Component: Stems; Habitus
  • Description of the Concern: Inhibited growth dynamics due to insufficient photoperiods.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Short light/dark cycles disrupt photoperiodic signaling and gibberellin pathways.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Morrow and Wheeler 1996.
  • Preventive Measures: Avoid light/dark cycles shorter than 24 hours.
  • Reactive Measures: Reprogram photoperiod to biologically appropriate cycles.

Picture source: Own sketch

Concern ID: HM-006 — Premature leaf senescence
  • Affected System Component: Habitus
  • Description of the Concern: Early aging, potentially leaf loss and stunted growth due to too low red/far-red ratio (simulating shade conditions).
  • Underlying Mechanism: Phytochrome-mediated stress signaling induces senescence pathways.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Wassenaar et al. 2022.
  • Preventive Measures: Ensure species-specific light ratios and avoid canopy shading.
  • Reactive Measures: Adjust light composition to increase red/far-red ratio.

Picture source: Wassenaar et al. 2022

Concern ID: HM-007 — Growth anomalies due to circadian desynchronization
  • Affected System Component: Habitus; Roots
  • Description of the Concern: Abnormal growth patterns resulting from disruption of circadian rhythms.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Desynchronized circadian plant clocks interfere with hormone regulation and metabolism.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Ruts et al. 2012.
  • Preventive Measures: Use crop specific light settings that match natural day/night cycles or established plant chronotypes.
  • Reactive Measures: Reset photoperiod synchronization and monitor response.

Picture source: Ruts et al. 2012; Text from Saskia Weser

Concern ID: HM-008 — Algae growth in nutrient solution
  • Affected System Component: Root zone; Nutrient delivery system
  • Description of the Concern: Proliferation of algae in light-exposed nutrient reservoirs with stagnant flow.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Photosynthetic contamination competing for nutrients and oxygen.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Schwarz and Gross 2015.
  • Preventive Measures: Ensure covered nutrient solution streams; Maintain fluid motion.
  • Reactive Measures: Flush irrigation systems and remove (wipe) biofilms.

Improper temperatures
Concern ID: HM-009 — Metabolic slowdown
  • Affected System Component: Habitus
  • Description of the Concern: Decrease in physiological activity due to cold-induced nutrient uptake limitations.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Reduced enzymatic function and slowed metabolic transport at suboptimal temperatures.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Nievola et al. 2017.
  • Preventive Measures: Maintain temperatures above species-specific critical thresholds.
  • Reactive Measures: Adjust thermal settings promptly and monitor nutrient assimilation rates.
Concern ID: HM-010 — Leaf burns and tissue damage
  • Affected System Component: Leaves; Blooms.
  • Description of the Concern: To high temperatures for too long can lead to leaf spots or a burn along the margin of the leaf.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Thermal stress exceeding water supply.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Rose and Swift 2009.
  • Preventive Measures: Ensure sufficient spacing between lamps and plant tissues; use heat shielding.
  • Reactive Measures: Reduce light intensity; Increase humidity to buffer temperature peaks.

Both picture sources: www.treloarroses.com accessed 07/2025

Concern ID: HM-011 — Flower abortion
  • Affected System Component: Blooms
  • Description of the Concern: Aborted blooms due to temperature fluctuations beyond reproductive tolerance range.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Heat or cold shock during critical phases of bloom development.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Maynard and Guan 2024.
  • Preventive Measures: Stabilize temperature within optimal range during reproductive phases.
  • Reactive Measures: Restore temperature stability and monitor for compensatory blooming.

Picture source: vegcropshotline.org accessed 07/2025

Improper air humidity
Concern ID: HM-012 — Increased microbial infection risk due to condensation on plants or hardware
  • Affected System Component: Plant surfaces; Subsystem hardware.
  • Description of the Concern: Water condensation on surfaces triggers proliferation of fungal pathogens that might infect plants, especially if they are already weakened.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Warm, moisture-laden air comes in contact with cooler surfaces causing the air to reach its dew point and release water as surface moisture (= condensation).
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from Nisar et al. 2020.
  • Preventive Measures: Maintain sufficient temperature gradients; Use Mollier-h-x-diagram as dew point control.
  • Reactive Measures: Reduce relative humidity (rH); Increase temperature slightly to eliminate dew point conditions; Wipe condensed water from surfaces. If microbial infection observed, apply diagnostic procedure + targeted measures on identified pathogen via protocol.
Concern ID: HM-013 — Increased microbial infection risk due to long-lasting high humidity and poor air circulation
  • Affected System Component: Plants; Atmosphere management system
  • Description of the Concern: Long-lasting highly moist air leads to anoxic microclimates promoting fungal growth on plants.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Microorgansims proliferate in high rH settings with poor air circulation.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Carisse and Kushalappa 1992, Mislivec and Tuite 1970 and Romero et al. 2022.
  • Preventive Measures: Optimize airflow across plant surfaces and regulate humidity.
  • Reactive Measures: Enhance air circulation. If microbial infection observed, apply diagnostic procedure + targeted measures on identified pathogen via protocol.
Concern ID: HM-014 — Wilting due to low relative Humidity (rH)
  • Affected System Component: Plants; Atmosphere management- and irrigation system
  • Description of the Concern: Excessive transpiration causes dehydration, turgor loss and ultimately senescence.
  • Underlying Mechanism: When rH is low, the vapor pressure deficit (VPD) between leaf interior and surrounding air increases. This accelerates transpiration rates beyond the plant’s water uptake capacity via roots and causes water imbalance at cellular level.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Spomer and Tibbitts 1996.
  • Preventive Measures: Maintain minimum rH above 30% and synchronize with irrigation cycles and temperature.
  • Reactive Measures: Increase rH; Adjust irrigation intervals; Lower temperature short-term.
Concern ID: HM-015 — Tipburn
  • Affected System Component: Tips of leaves; Atmosphere management- and nutrient delivery system
  • Description of the Concern: Little brown spots on leaf tips due to calcium deficiency.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Insufficient stomatal closure in high humidity during day cycle reducing calcium transport.
  • Source of the Concern: Experienced, documented by Morrow and Wheeler 1996, Schuerger et al. 2022 and Barta and Tibbitts 1991.
  • Preventive Measures: Regulate humidity by day and enhance transpiration cues.
  • Reactive Measures: Adjust humidity dynamics across light/dark cycle; Ensure nutrient uptake efficiency.

Picture source: greenhousecanada.com accessed 07/2025

Concern ID: HM-016 — Blossom-end rot
  • Affected System Component: Blooms and bottom of fruits; Atmosphere management system; Nutrient delivery system
  • Description of the Concern: Necrotic tissue at the bottom of fruits.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Poor transpiration flow hinder calcium reaching fruit extremities.
  • Source of the Concern: Experienced, documented by Morrow and Wheeler 1996 and Schuerger et al. 2022.
  • Preventive Measures: Maintain balanced transpiration via controlled humidity.
  • Reactive Measures: Similar to HM-015 — Tipburn: Optimize humidity and ensure calcium availability.

Picture source: greenlife.co.ke accessed 07/2025

Improper atmosphere composition
Concern ID: HM-017 — Weakened plants due to ethylene stress
  • Affected System Component: Plants; Atmosphere management system
  • Description of the Concern: Ethylene levels in air above 50 ppb trigger premature senescence, flower abortion and reduced fruit set, Leaf epinasty and increase plant's susceptibility to infections.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Ethylene-sensitive signaling pathways alter plant hormonal balance and tissue development, which reduces resistance to microbial attack.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented Wheeler et al. 1996 and Wheeler et al. 2004.
  • Preventive Measures: Integrate ethylene permanganate filters or catalytic burners; Monitor gas composition regularly.
  • Reactive Measures: Increase air exchange rate. If microbial infection observed, apply diagnostic procedure + targeted measures on identified pathogen via protocol.

Picture source: Wheeler et al. 2004

Concern ID: HM-018 — Growth reduction from CO₂ deficiency
  • Affected System Component: Plants; CO₂-delivery system
  • Description of the Concern: Reduced vegetative and reproductive performance due to low ambient CO₂ concentration.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Limitation of photosynthesis and carbon assimilation processes.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Peet and Krizek 1996.
  • Preventive Measures: Supply CO₂ and maintain optimal levels (1000–2000 ppm) with monitoring regularly.
  • Reactive Measures: Inject CO₂ and adjust ventilation rates as needed.
Concern ID: HM-019 — Physiological inhibition from high CO₂ levels
  • Affected System Component: Human/plant module air interaction.
  • Description of the Concern: Human activity within plant module increases CO₂ levels rapidly, affecting physiological processes such as transpiration and photosynthesis. Leaf roling or deformation, chlorosis or chlorotic mottling of leaves, or general growth reduction may occurr.
  • Underlying Mechanism: CO₂ oversaturation disrupts stomatal function.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Peet and Krizek 1996 and Wheeler et al. 1993.
  • Preventive Measures: Minimize human interaction in growth module or use special vaccuum/mask systems as invented by Stewart and Bernier 1994.
  • Reactive Measures: Increase air exchange rate to vent excess CO₂.
Concern ID: HM-020 — Chemical stress from air pollutants
  • Affected System Component: Plants; Construction materials.
  • Description of the Concern: Pollutants from construction materials such as plastic, silicone, paint or glue can be phytotoxic by causing unspecific growth disorders.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Released toxic compounds or byproducts affect plants physiology.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Tibbitts 1996.
  • Preventive Measures: Use certified construction materials; Use activated charchoal filters; Choose resilient plant varieties; Increase resilience of plants via antioxidant supply (e.g. ascorbate, glutathione-inducing fertilizers) or plant hormones (e.g. salicylic acid, ABA).
  • Reactive Measures: Enhance air exchange.
Subhazard 1.2: Inadequate handling
Incorrect pruning
Concern ID: HM-021 — Sink-source imbalances
  • Affected System Component: Plants; Pruning procedure
  • Description of the Concern: Wrong pruning (cutting to much, cutting at wrong time) disrupts biomass allocation and resilience, which can cause delayed flowering or fruit development.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Disproportionate sink/source ratio disturbs photosynthate distribution.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from Pawar and Rana 2019 and Ryan et al. 2018.
  • Preventive Measures: Provide pruning guidelines based on plant-specific sink/source dynamics and phenological stages.
  • Reactive Measures: Restore balance by targeted pruning or regrowth stimulation.
Concern ID: HM-022 — Increased microbial infection risk due to contaminated tools
  • Affected System Component: Plants; Tools
  • Description of the Concern: Improper sanitation of cutting tools might transfer pathogens from infected plants to noninfected plants.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Pathogens introduced via cutting wounds under favorable conditions.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Bausher 2013.
  • Preventive Measures: Sterilize tools after use.
  • Reactive Measures: If microbial infection observed, apply diagnostic procedure + targeted measures on identified pathogen via protocol.
Improper pollination/fertilization
Concern ID: HM-023 — No or reduced fruit set, misshaped fruits
  • Affected System Component: Plant's generative system; Pollination activities
  • Description of the Concern: Failure of proper fruit development due to inefficient pollination or incomplete fertilization
  • Underlying Mechanism: Uncontrolled or ineffective pollen transfer avoid or disrupt fertilization.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Grace and Marshall 1992 and Lord and Russell 2002.
  • Preventive Measures: Use parthenocarpic species (if possible); Apply manual pollination via brushs or through air circulation; Ensure optimal environmental conditions in pollination phase (rH and temperature influence pollen quality and fertilization).
  • Reactive Measures: Repeat pollination immediately; Replant if no improvement.
Concern ID: HM-024 — Seedless fruit in long-term missions
  • Affected System Component: Plant's generative system; Long-term reproduction stability
  • Description of the Concern: Parthenocarpy prevents seed development, which might be problematic in long-term systems relying on generative propagation.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Lack of fertilization in parthenocarpic cultivars blocks seed formation.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred, based on Grace and Marshall 1992 and Lord and Russell 2002.
  • Preventive Measures: Use open-pollinated, non-hybrid cultivars for long-term sustainability.
  • Reactive Measures: As in HM-023.
Mechanical damage
Concern ID: HM-025 — Broken plant parts
  • Affected System Component: Plant parts; Physical interaction with crew or robotics; Spatial configuration of growth chamber
  • Description of the Concern: Physical damage to stems, leaves, or roots during maintenance activites or due to low space; Overlapping leaves.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Broken plant parts affect sink/source balances (see HM-021) and cause potential infection entry points, overlapping leaves reduce light capture and gas exchange.
  • Source of the Concern: Experienced.
  • Preventive Measures: Ensure proper space for plants ifself and crew for maintenance activites; Use supporting structures for plants.
  • Reactive Measures: Straight plants to ensure proper growth space and lighting; Remove damaged parts (sharp cut); If necessary, restore sink/source balance by targeted additional pruning; If microbial infection observed, apply diagnostic procedure + targeted measures on identified pathogen via protocol.
Concern ID: HM-026 — Damage during transplanting
  • Affected System Component: Roots; Transplanting activities
  • Description of the Concern: Relocation of plants (from germination setup to vegetative+generative growth setup) can damage roots physically or induce chemical stress.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Damaged roots interrupt proper water and nutrient uptake; Change of root environment might pose a higher risk of microbial infection.
  • Source of the Concern: Experienced.
  • Preventive Measures: Prepare transplanting process in advance to limit exposure time of roots outside of intended root zone; Minimize invasive actions (for example touching roots); Ensure having enough back-up plants if a plant fail to adapt to new environment.
  • Reactive Measures: Monitor plants after transplanting; Replace with back up plants if a plant fails to develop.
Subhazard 1.3: Irrigation/Nutrition
Watering inconsistencies
Concern ID: HM-027 — Hypoxia due to overwatering
  • Affected System Component: Roots; Irrigation system
  • Description of the Concern: Roots surrounded long-term by water layers can lead to stunted growth, leaf curling, chlorosis and a higher suscebtility to microbial infections. Especially relevant in μg conditions (see AG-001).
  • Underlying Mechanism: Water accumulation in root area supresses oxygen supply, which is important for respiration. Also causes nutrient deficiencies.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Massa et al. 2017 and Hoehn et al. 2000.
  • Preventive Measures: Track water supply, ensure proper aeration around roots.
  • Reactive Measures: Remove water manually; Monitor for improvement. If microbial infection observed, apply diagnostic procedure + targeted measures on identified pathogen via protocol.

Picture source: Massa et al.2017

Concern ID: HM-028 — Fruit cracking
  • Affected System Component: Fruits, Irrigation system
  • Description of the Concern: Rapid water intake after dry periods can cause cracking in the fruit.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Elasticity threshold of skin exceeded due to osmotic imbalance.
  • Source of the Concern: Experienced; Documented by Morrow and Wheeler 2006.
  • Preventive Measures: Selection of nonsusceptible cultivars; Consistency in watering, Good calcium nutrition; Possibly the use of calcium or gibberellic acid sprays.
  • Reactive Measures: -

Both picture sources: vegetables.bayer.com accessed 07/2025.

Concern ID: HM-029 — Drought wilt
  • Affected System Component: Plants; Irrigation system
  • Description of the Concern: Crispy, wilting plants with stopped growth due to water deficiency.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Transpiration exceeds water uptake, which leads to tugor loss and cellular collapse.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Farooq et al. 2012 and Yang et al. 2020.
  • Preventive Measures: Ensure sufficient irrigation in balance with environmental conditions and needs.
  • Reactive Measures: Increase water volume or frequency until recovery observed.

Both picture sources: blogs.cornell.edu accessed 07/2025.

Improper nutrient supply
Concern ID: HM-030 — Nutrient deficiencies
  • Affected System Component: Plants; Nutrient delivery system
  • Description of the Concern: Essential elements missing or imbalanced causing visible plant stress such as chlorosis, necrosis, malformations and many more.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Plants rely on nutrients to carry out essential functions like photosynthesis and cell division, to produce energy or short: to grow.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Stevens et al. 2002.
  • Preventive Measures: Ensure proper nutrient mixture; Calibrate concentration via EC; Ensure optimal pH; Use reverse osmosis water.
  • Reactive Measures: Apply targeted supplementation based on deficiency diagnosis.

Picture source: plantnutrition.com accessed 07/2025

Picture source: Stevens et al. 2002

Concern ID: HM-031 — Nutrient toxicities
  • Affected System Component: Plants; Nutrient delivery system
  • Description of the Concern: Oversupply causes various visible stress responses such as chlorosis or malformations.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Oversupply of certain nutrients causes toxic buildup or supression of intake of other essential nutrients. Closely linked to HM0031.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Fan et al. 2021 and Mithare 2020.
  • Preventive Measures: Balance nutrient input; Monitore EC and pH levels.
  • Reactive Measures: Dilute nutrient solution or flush system with water.

Picture source: Mithare 2021

Hazard 2: Hostile/Closed Environment
Subhazard 2.1: Improper resource management
Concern ID: HCE-001 — Shortage of critical operation materials
  • Affected System Component: Material supply chain
  • Description of the Concern: Shortages of fertilizers, substrates, spare parts, etc. can affect plant health indirectly.
  • Underlying Mechanism: False calculations, unseen needs or malfunctioning recycling loops.
  • Source of the Concern: Theoretical
  • Preventive Measures: Use of precise calculation models; Maintain backup stock.
  • Reactive Measures: Redistribution if possible; Initiate rationing or emergency sourcing protocols; Employ creative solutions to compensate missing resource.
Concern ID: HCE-002 — Waste/gas-related phytotoxicity
  • Affected System Component: Waste management
  • Description of the Concern: Organic waste releases decomposition gases which can cause root damage
  • Underlying Mechanism: Altered pH of nutrient solution and reduced dissolved oxygen levels from released toxic gases like ammonia or hydrogen sulfide.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from Improper atmosphere composition in Hazard 1.
  • Preventive Measures: Strict hygiene: Immediate organic waste removal; Ensure proper aeration.
  • Reactive Measures: Flush nutrient delivery system to reduce gas composition; Improve oxygenation.
Concern ID: HCE-003 — Microbial proliferation from harvest waste
  • Affected System Component: Waste management
  • Description of the Concern: Organic waste promotes microbial growth which can harm plants when they infect them.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Saprophytes proliferate on dead organic matter.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from Gitelson and Lisovsky 2008 and Salisbury et al. 1997.
  • Preventive Measures: Same as in HCE-002.
  • Reactive Measures: Locate and remove disposed organic waste isolated from plant growth area. If microbial infection observed, apply diagnostic procedure + targeted measures on identified pathogen via protocol.
Subhazard 2.2: Regolith and dust contamination
Concern ID: HCE-004 — Toxic perchlorates from martian regolith
  • Affected System Component: Plant metabolism; Microbial life; Habitat design
  • Description of the Concern: Contamination with toxic perchlorates or unknown compounds might affect plants an microbial life.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Toxicity from Martian regolith (e.g. perchlorates, heavy metals).
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from Wang et al. 2025 , Misra et al. 2021 and Rothschild 2024 .
  • Preventive Measures: Use habitat airlocks to prevent general entry; Remove perchlorates via biocatalysis/filtration; Regular cleaning.
  • Reactive Measures: Ongoing research – implement current best practices where possible.
Concern ID: HCE-005 — Reduced photosynthesis due to "normal" dust
  • Affected System Component: Plant surfaces and stomata; Module interactions
  • Description of the Concern: Blocked light absorption and gas exchange leading to growth disruptions.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Excessive amounts of "normal" dust entering the plant module from other modules (e.g. living module) may set down on plants and block photosynthesis
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from Shah et al. 2019 and Chaurasia et al. 2022.
  • Preventive Measures: Regular cleaning; Air filtration and air locks between modules.
  • Reactive Measures: Manual or automated plant surface cleaning.
Concern ID: HCE-006 — Hardware/system malfunction due to "normal" dust
  • Affected System Component: Subsystem filters and moving parts (fans, pumps, robotics)
  • Description of the Concern: Mechanical failure of subsystems from dust clogging or abrasion might affect plant health indirectly .
  • Underlying Mechanism: Excessive amounts of "normal" dust entering the plant module from other modules (e.g. living module) may set down on and in subsystems.
  • Source of the Concern: Theoretical
  • Preventive Measures: As in HCE-005.
  • Reactive Measures: Manual or automated hardware cleaning.
Subhazard 2.3: Entry and accumulation of harmful organisms
Concern ID: HCE-007 — Microorganisms
  • Affected System Component: Internal plant module; Module interaction; Vectors.
  • Description of the Concern: Uncontrolled accumulation of harmful bacteria, funghi or viruses increase the risk of infections causing severe plant diseases like .......
  • Underlying Mechanism: Entry of pathogens into plant module through vectors like air, water, crew, tools that move between modules or shared water cycle. Accumulation due to favourable conditions in an isolated environment and suppression of supportive microbes. Infections through open wounds on plants, weakend plant conditions, roots.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Salisbury et al. 1997, Gitelson and Lisovsky 2008, Gonzales et al. 1996, Pierson 2001 and Schuerger 1998.
  • Preventive Measures: Preflight microbial checks and sanitization; In-flight monitoring (air, surfaces, water); Use of air filters (HEPA) and slightly higher air pressure in plant module to prevent air entry; Separated water cycles of plant- and other modules; Decontamination of water/nutrient solution (e.g. ozone filtration or UV light exposure); Protocols for maintaining general hygiene in plant module; Use of Plant resistence support-strategies (plant strengtheners, Mykorrhiza); Crop rotation; Bacterization; Environmental manipulation to supress infection risk; Organization of plant cultivation into separate compartments to better isolate affected areas if necessary.
  • Reactive Measures: Preventive management strategies can also be employed as interventive management strategies. Additionally: If microbial infection observed, apply diagnostic procedure + targeted measures on identified pathogen via protocol.
Concern ID: HCE-008 — Pests - Relevant for missions with insect production
  • Affected System Component: Module interaction; Vectors.
  • Description of the Concern: Insects can harm plants through eating plant parts, sap-sucking, mining and boring and disease transmission.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Entry of insects from insect module into plant module through vectors like air, crew or tools that move between modules. Accumulation due to favourable conditions in an isolated environment and a lack of natural competitors.
  • Source of the Concern: Theoretical
  • Preventive Measures: Preflight pest checks and sanitization; In-flight monitoring (air, surfaces, plants); Use of air filters (HEPA) and slightly higher air pressure in plant module to prevent air entry; Protocols for maintaining general hygiene in plant module; Strategies for shared tools and crew members working in both facilities (for example exchange of clothes between modules or use of oversuits, cleaning of all tools, etc.); Use of Plant resistence support-strategies (plant strengtheners, Mykorrhiza); Crop rotation; Organization of plant cultivation into separate compartments to better isolate affected areas if necessary.
  • Reactive Measures: Preventive management strategies can also be employed as interventive management strategies. Additionally: If pest damage observed, apply diagnostic procedure + targeted measures on identified pest via protocol.
Subhazard 2.4: Material decay in long-term missions
Concern ID: HCE-009 — Microplastic contamination of water cycles and plants itself
  • Affected System Component: Irrigation system, plants
  • Description of the Concern: Plastics degrade over time, contaminating water with particles that might build up in plants.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Aging of plastic materials under mission conditions.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from Roy et al. 2023 , Li et al. 2020 , Schwab et al. 2020 and UNEP Foresight Brief 029.
  • Preventive Measures: Use non-plastic components (PTFE, stainless steel); Particle monitoring.
  • Reactive Measures: Physical filtration; Part replacement; Material upgrades.

Picture source: Roy et al. 2023

Concern ID: HCE-010 — Leaks in pipes and mechanical connections
  • Affected System Component: Irrigation system
  • Description of the Concern: Corrosion- and fatigue induced system damage. Moisture loss, pressure loss.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Material fatigue from pressure, vibration, thermal cycling and corrosion through chronic moisture and ionizing radiation.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from John et al. 2024 , Xu et al. 2015 and Kaneko et al. 2015.
  • Preventive Measures: Use fatigue- and corrosion resistant materials; Simulate stress before mission starts; Install leak sensors.
  • Reactive Measures: Isolate defective parts; Patch with epoxy/self-healing polymers; Use 3D-printed spares.
Concern ID: HCE-011 — Gradual performance loss of CEA-subsystems (for example lighting system)
  • Affected System Component: CEA-subsystems and sensors
  • Description of the Concern: lighting components and other sensitive components degrade slowly over time and might affect plant health indirectly as they not work as intended.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Depending on the CEA-subsystem. For lighting: Lumen‑Depreciation (= decrease in lumen output that occurs as a lamp is operated) due to heat, moisture and dust deposits.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from Narendran and Gu 2005 and U.S. Department of Energy 2009.
  • Preventive Measures: Use industrial-grade, modular lamps; Protect from deposits, Monitor output.
  • Reactive Measures: Replace panels or adjust light settings according performance loss (for example add a second panel).
Subhazard 2.5: Evolutionary concerns in long-term missions
Concern ID: HCE-012 — DNA damage, mutations, altered gene expression, sterility
  • Affected System Component: Plant genomes
  • Description of the Concern: Decline in fitness (mutation load) through genetic changes caused by ionizing radiation exposure (also see Hazard 4 — Chronic ionzing radiation).
  • Underlying Mechanism: Ionizing radiation damages plants by causing water radiolysis, which generates reactive oxygen species (ROS) that attack cellular components, including DNA, leading to genetic damage, chromosomal aberrations, and potentially faulty repair of double-strand breaks, all of which can impair plant growth and long-term species development.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from chernobyl incident. Reported by Ludovici et al. 2020 .
  • Preventive Measures: Active and passive radiation shielding; Use of so called radioresistent varieties; Clonal propagation of viable lines (via in-vitro culture for example); Regular genetic monitoring via sequencing.
  • Reactive Measures: Applying gene editing (e.g. CRISPR/Cas) to repair damaged DNA. Only helps population/next generation but not plant individuum itself.
Concern ID: HCE-013 — Epingenetic drift and loss of traits
  • Affected System Component: Gene expression regulation
  • Description of the Concern: Loss of useful features through epigenetic silencing and drift and inheritance of stable but undesirable conditions (like reduced fertility, growth inhibition) through artificial CEA environment.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Selection pressure resulting from controlled environmental conditions (artificial lighting, elevated CO₂ concentrations, absence of natural environmental fluctuations) can trigger epigenetic changes. Over time, these changes may accumulate (epigenetic drift), leading to altered gene expression patterns and potential loss of desirable traits.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from studies on plant responses to long-term environmental stress and clonal propagation, which show that stable artificial conditions can lead to persistent epigenetic modifications (Springer and Schmitz 2017, Dar et al. 2022).
  • Preventive Measures: Use epigenetically stable lines; Control environment.
  • Reactive Measures: Epigenetic monitoring for example DNA Methylation Analysis; Resetting via tissue culture; Selective propagation of favorable epigenetic states.
Concern ID: HCE-014 — Reduced genetic diversity
  • Affected System Component: Seed stock and reproduction management
  • Description of the Concern: Reduced adaptability, increased susceptibility to disease, or inbreeding depression due to low genetic diversity can compromise yield stability.
  • Underlying Mechanism: In small and isolated plant populations, genetic diversity declines because of random genetic drift, bottlenecks, inbreeding, and the lack of gene flow as new alleles cannot enter the population which limits the evolutionary potential.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from Ellstrand and Elam 2003, Neaves et al. 2015, Le Pajolec et al. 2022 and Khoury et al. 2022.
  • Preventive Measures: According to Kumari et al. 2025 use a genetically diverse initial seed pool; Cryopreserving of seeds, tissue or embryos; In-vitro propagation; Regular genetic monitoring via sequencing.
  • Reactive Measures: According to Wolter et al. 2019 cross-breeding; Gene editing (for example CRISPR/Cas) to repair or enhance diversity.
Concern ID: HCE-015 — Microbial hyper-evolution
  • Affected System Component: Microbial ecosystem in long term missions
  • Description of the Concern: In closed or isolated environments, microbial populations can evolve unchecked, potentially giving rise to hyper-aggressive or resistant plant pathogens.
  • Underlying Mechanism: The lack of ecological regulation and gene flow in closed systems facilitates rapid adaptation through mutation and selection, often favoring virulence or resistance traits without natural constraints.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from Lenski's E. coli long-term evolution experiment (the-ltee).
  • Preventive Measures: Use biodiversity-promoting practices, implement strict biocontainment protocols, and regularly monitor microbial populations to prevent dangerous evolutionary trajectories.
  • Reactive Measures: Apply adaptive control measures such as rotating biocontrol agents, deploying counter-selection pressure, and genetically modifying crops or microbes to suppress pathogenicity in emergent strains.
Hazard 3: Altered Gravity
Subhazard 3.1: Altered water- and air movement
Concern ID: AG-001 — Hypoxia due to microgravity
  • Affected System Component: Roots; Irrigation system
  • Description of the Concern: In microgravity, water tends to cling to root surfaces due to increased surface tension, which blocks oxygen diffusion and causes root hypoxia. Stress responses are stunted growth, leaf curling, chlorosis and a higher suscebtility to microbial infections (related to HM-028).
  • Underlying Mechanism: Absence of gravitational drainage leads to water accumulation, suppressing passive gas exchange at the root surface.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Levine 2021 and Massa et al. 2017.
  • Preventive Measures: Develop passive or semi-passive irrigation systems adapted to microgravity, like pressure-regulated pumps as used in APH or capillary wicking as used in PONDS; Relevant could be a consideration of an artificial gravity cultivation environment realised by using centrifugal force through rotation.
  • Reactive Measures: Remove water manually; Adjust irrigation amount; Modify root substrate (if used) or wick system; Monitor oxygenation. If microbial infection observed, apply diagnostic procedure + targeted measures on identified pathogen via protocol.
Concern ID: AG-002 — Non-uniform humidity distribution
  • Affected System Component: Air circulation; Atmosphere management system.
  • Description of the Concern: Partial humidity hotspots or dry zones within plant module can lead to partial increased or decreased transpiration rates affecting biomass production negatively.
  • Underlying Mechanism: In the absence of gravity-driven convection, water vapor accumulates unevenly, leading to stagnant air and microclimates within the module.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Musgrave et al. 1997, Monje et al. 2003, Heinse et al. 2007, Kitaya et al. 2010 and Blancaflor et al. 2023.
  • Preventive Measures: Use ventilation (for example axial or radial fans) to actively circulate air in all module areas.
  • Reactive Measures: Adjust fan intensity and direction to redistribute air and homogenize humidity.
Concern ID: AG-003 — Reduced evaporative cooling
  • Affected System Component: Leaves; Air circulation; Atmosphere management system.
  • Description of the Concern: Reduced evaporative cooling increase thermal stress in plants which can lead to disruptions in photosynthesis, cell damage or stunted growth.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Plants cool themselves via evapotranspiration. Under μg, a lack of convection (= vertical airflow) limits this cooling which raises tissue temperature.
  • Source of the Concern: Same as in AG-002.
  • Preventive Measures: As in AG-002; Consider location of fans near leaf area.
  • Reactive Measures: Monitor leaf temperatures; Adjust airflow or lighting to reduce thermal load.
Subhazard 3.2: Hormonal, developmental and reproductive concerns
Concern ID: AG-004 — Thinner cell walls
  • Affected System Component: Cells
  • Description of the Concern: Plants grown in microg have thinner and less rigid cell walls which decreases mechanical stability against physical stress, weakens the plant’s ability to perceive and respond to environmental or mechanical signals, increases susceptibility to pathogens and impaires water regulation (cells might collapse when they cannot withstand turgor pressure).
  • Underlying Mechanism: No mechanical stimulus to strengthen the cell walls which results in an impaired biogenesis of the cell wall polymers lignin, cellulose, and hemicellulose.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Hoson et al. 2009, Hoson 2014, Totsline et al. 2023, Johnson et al. 2017 and Wakabayashi et al. 2015.
  • Preventive Measures: Currently not available. Further research required, but a study by Paul et al. 2017 suggests that the genetic and environmental adaptation of plants to micorgravity (on costs of biomassproduction) is not necessarily essential for survival and can be made significantly more efficient through targeted genetic modifications and environmental manipulation (especially light settings).
  • Reactive Measures: Currently not available. Further research required.

Concern ID: AG-005 — Altered cytokinin distribution
  • Affected System Component: Cells
  • Description of the Concern: An abnormal cytokinin distribution can disrupt cell division, organ development, hormone balance (especially with Auxin), and stress resilience, ultimately compromising overall plant growth and health.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Cytokinins are key regulators of plant growth, development, and stress responses and in microgravity, cytokinin is distributed uneven within the plant.
  • Source of the Concern: Altered cytokinin distribution documented by Ferl and Paul 2016, resulting effects on plant health documented by Werner et al. 2003, Müller and Sheen 2008 and Kieber and Schaller 2018.
  • Preventive Measures: Same as in AG-004.
  • Reactive Measures: Same as in AG-004.
Concern ID: AG-006 — Altered gene expression
  • Affected System Component: Cells
  • Description of the Concern: In microgravity, plants exhibit signs of chronic cellular stress, resulting in reduced growth, developmental abnormalities, increased vulnerability to environmental and pathogenic stress, and potentially impaired reproductive capacity.
  • Underlying Mechanism: absence of gravity disrupts core cellular functions such as redox balance, protein quality control, and stress signal transduction, leading to the overproduction of reactive oxygen species (ROS), activation of heat shock proteins (HSPs), and widespread transcriptomic reprogramming, which collectively shift the plant’s physiological priorities from growth toward stress mitigation.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Paul et al. 2012, Kwon et al. 2015 , Johnson et al. 2017, Choi et al. 2019 and Zupanska et al. 2013.
  • Preventive Measures: Same as in AG-004.
  • Reactive Measures: Same as in AG-004.
Concern ID: AG-007 — Lack of pollen dispersal or inefficient transfer
  • Affected System Component: Reproductive organs; Air circulation system.
  • Description of the Concern: Without gravity, pollen does not settle naturally onto stigmas, reducing fertilization rates.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Gravity is a passive vector for pollen and without it, dispersal is impaired.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from AG-002.
  • Preventive Measures: Use airflow or vibrational techniques to promote pollen movement.
  • Reactive Measures:Perform manual pollination with brushes or controlled airflow at anthesis.
Hazard 4: Chronic Ionizing Radiation
Subhazard 4.1: Genetic, Metabolic, and Reproductive Concerns
Concern ID: CIR-001 — Genetic Instability/Mutations across generations
  • Affected System Component: Plant's genom; Propagation
  • Description of the Concern: Ionizing radiation exposure can lead to heritable genomic instability, manifesting as phenotypic abnormalities, reduced seed viability, and long-term variability in growth and reproduction which ultimately compromises the genetic reliability and health of cultivated plant lines.
  • Underlying Mechanism: High-energy particles (e.g. HZE ions) induce DNA damage which is often stably inherited across plant generations.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Tanaka et al. 2010, Kovalchuk 2000, Zimmermann et al. 1996, Mei et al. 1994 and Ou et al. 2009.
  • Preventive Measures: Physical shielding of module through construction materials like aluminium, water- and polymer-based radiation-proof layers as mentioned by Baiocco et al. 2018 and Cherkashina 2024; Underground construction of plant module on Moon or Mars as proposed by Cowley et al. 2016; Active shielding trough Magnetic fields, plasma shields or electrostatic systems to deflect IR as mentioned by Chowdhury 2023; (not applicable yet, still in research); Biotechnical strategies: Use of radiation-resistant plant species (inferred from Volkova et al. 2021 and Geras'kin 2024); Use of GMOs or proteins with repair mechanisms or higher tolerance to IR (indicated by Szabla et al. 2024).
  • Reactive Measures: Genetic screening; Selection of unaffected specimens; Maybe biotechnical repair via GMOs or symbiotic microbes.
Concern ID: CIR-002 — Growth defects in exposed Individuum
  • Affected System Component: Individuum's chromosomal structure
  • Description of the Concern: Stop of the cell cycle, mitotic errors, accelerated cell aging or cell death, premature senescence and reduced germination induced by exposure to ionizing radiation.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Irreparable DNA double-strand breaks result in chromosomal aberrations which compromise mitosis and trigger cell death particularly in meristematic tissues (sensitivity is varying by tissue type, radiation LET, and plant genotype).
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Hase et al. 2002, Hase et al. 1999, Mei et al. 1994, Yokota et al. 2003, Yokota et al. 2007 and Shi et al. 2010.
  • Preventive Measures: As in CIR-001.
  • Reactive Measures: As in CIR-001.
Concern ID: CIR-003 — Increased susceptibility to disease
  • Affected System Component: Immune regulation.
  • Description of the Concern: Damage to genetic control systems might impair pathogen defense signaling.
  • Underlying Mechanism: As described in CIR-001 and 002.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from CIR-001 and 002.
  • Preventive Measures: As described in CIR-001.
  • Reactive Measures: As described in CIR-001.
Concern ID: CIR-004 — Reduced photosynthetic efficiency
  • Affected System Component: Chloroplasts and light-harvesting complex
  • Description of the Concern: Radiation damages chloroplast structures, especially PSII, which redcued photosynthesis.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Photon or particle impact leads to photoinhibition and pigment breakdown.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Angelini et al. 2001, Abe et al. 2002, Palamine et al. 2005 and Cheng and Chandlee 1999.
  • Preventive Measures: As described in CIR-001.
  • Reactive Measures: As described in CIR-001.
Subhazard 4.2: Material Decay
Concern ID: CIR-005 — Material fatigue and corrosion of hardware
  • Affected System Component: Structural and mechanical hardware (metals, composites)
  • Description of the Concern: Persistent exposure to space radiation and atomic oxygen causes microcracking, embrittlement and corrosion, which weakens hardware and reduces operational lifespan ultimately affecting plant cultivation compononents.
  • Underlying Mechanism: High-energy particles displace atoms and ionize materials, triggering radiation hardening, embrittlement (via microvoids, defect clusters), and ionization corrosion; atomic oxygen further oxidizes surfaces.
  • Source of the Concern: Documented by Sznajder et al. 2015, Willenshofer et al. 2022 and Abd El-Hameed 2022.
  • Preventive Measures: As referred in Subhazard 2.3.
  • Reactive Measures:As referred in Subhazard 2.3.
Concern ID: CIR-006 — Microbial imbalance
  • Affected System Component: Microbial ecosystems (for example rhizosphere)
  • Description of the Concern: Radiation selectively suppresses or eliminates microbial species which disrupts microbial balance, especially beneficial communities.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Unequal DNA repair or tolerance capacities among species shift microbial composition unfavorably.
  • Source of the Concern: Inferred from CIR-001 and 002.
  • Preventive Measures: Inoculate with resilient microorgansims.
  • Reactive Measures:Re-inoculate beneficial microorganisms; Adjust environmental conditions to support recolonization.

Environmental control-, irrigation- and lighting system failures

Concern ID: TC-003 — CO₂ level mismanagement
  • Affected System Component: Atmosphere management system especially gas delivery/regulation system

  • Description of the Concern: Potential plant dehydration due to pump failure.

Concern ID: TC-008 — Pressure loss in hydroponic circuits

Subhazard 5.2: Communication and remote operation concerns
Concern ID: TC-018 — Operational inconsistency due to differing horticultural practices or lack of communication
  • Affected System Component: Human communication and task coordination.
  • Description of the Concern: Differences in horticultural knowledge, preferences, or interpretation of protocols among personnel working in the plant module may result in conflicting care actions, leading to plant stress or health decline.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Inconsistent watering, pruning, or harvesting due to lack of clear communication or standardization or ignorance of protocols can cause over- or undertreatment of crops.
  • Source of the Concern: Experienced.
  • Preventive Measures: Develop standardized horticultural protocols and care checklists; Provide standardized training to ensure a common understanding of best practices; Document completed actions and decisions; Encourage open communication and collaborative problem-solving for care-related decisions.
  • Reactive Measures: Review recent actions and plant health indicators to identify discrepancies; Briefing for horticultural crew to clarify misunderstandings; Adjust care protocols based on feedback and observed outcomes; Assign clear responsibilities to reduce overlapping or contradictory actions.
Concern ID: TC-019 — Delayed responses
  • Affected System Component: Communication pathway between on-site (plant module) and off-site (earth control)
  • Description of the Concern: A technical communication lag between horticultural operators in the plant module and Earth-based mission control can lead to misinterpretation of intended real time data and delayed responses, especially in time-sensitive or emergency situations.
  • Underlying Mechanism: Communication delays can result from signal transmission latency (especially on deep space missions or due to orbital position), bandwidth limitations (which causes slower or prioritized data transfer), Data loss (see also TC-018) or a asynchronic work schedules (which means that earth operators and on-site operators are not available simultaneously to clarify issues artok).
  • Source of the Concern: Documented
  • Preventive Measures: Predefine clear decision protocols and thresholds for autonomous on-site action in case of delayed guidance; Provide on-site crew with emergency training; Synchronize working schedules between off-site and on-side; Use multiple data streams.
  • Reactive Measures: Use redundant communication methods (for example alternative data links or emergency channels). If delays occur: prioritize high-risk plant health data stream; for retransmission; Use temporary autonomous mode where the on-site crew makes decisions based on protocols; After: Review affected time periods post-incident to update decision trees and improve resilience.
